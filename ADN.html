Franks (Lower/Middle Rhine, Late Antiquity–Early Middle Ages)
Closest distances
Frank (6.57), +Nemetes (4.073), +Quadi (4.46), +Pannonian (4.89)
Language/Culture
West Germanic confederation (Salian & Ripuarian branches), strongly Roman-influenced by the 5th c.

Who and where

  • Origin along the lower and middle Rhine (modern Netherlands, Belgium, western Germany).
  • Foederati and foes of the Roman Empire; later founded the Merovingian kingdom in Gaul.

Population size

As a confederation in late antiquity likely several hundred thousand people spread across the Rhine frontier and northern Gaul.

Interactions

  • With Rome: raiding, service as federates, then rulers of northern Gaul.
  • With neighbors: Saxons, Alemanni, Thuringians, Burgundians; heavy admixture with Gallo‑Romans.

What happened

By the 6th–8th centuries the Franks dominated much of Gaul and western Germany, evolving into the Carolingian Empire.

Nemetes (Upper Rhine, Roman era)
Closest distances
Nemetes (8.127), +Frank (4.073), +Cherusci (4.582)
Identity
Small Rhine‑frontier tribe, culturally Celtic–Germanic; capital at Noviomagus of the Nemetes (near modern Speyer).

Who and where

  • Lived on the Upper Rhine opposite the Palatinate; incorporated into the Roman province of Germania Superior.
  • Known from Roman sources and inscriptions; allied to Rome at various times.

Population size

Likely a small civitas on the order of tens of thousands.

Interactions

  • Trade and military service under Rome; neighbors included Vangiones, Triboci, and later Alemanni and Franks.

What happened

Gradual Romanization; by late antiquity the Nemetes were absorbed into Alemannic and then Frankish populations along the Rhine.

Quadi (Moravia and western Slovakia, Roman imperial period)
Closest distances
Quadi (8.759), +Cherusci (4.134), +Frank (4.46)
Identity
Suebian (Germanic) group closely allied with the Marcomanni on Rome’s Danube frontier.

Who and where

  • Based along the upper Morava/March River basin (Czech Republic/Slovakia).
  • Well attested during the 1st–4th centuries CE.

Population size

Regional tribal society, probably in the low tens to hundreds of thousands across allied groups.

Interactions

  • Frequent conflict and treaties with Rome; central actors in the Marcomannic Wars (2nd century CE).
  • Contacts with Sarmatians and other steppe groups; mobility increased during the Migration Period.

What happened

By the 5th century the Quadi disappear as a distinct name, their people folding into broader Suebi and other migrating groups.

Cherusci (Weser region, early Roman era)
Closest distances
Cherusci (7.64), +Quadi (4.134), +Nemetes (4.582)
Identity
Northern Germanic tribe famous for defeating Roman legions in 9 CE under Arminius.

Who and where

  • Homeland around the Weser River (Lower Saxony, Germany).
  • Alliances and rivalries with neighboring Chatti, Bructeri, and others.

Population size

Likely a small to medium tribal group, perhaps in the tens of thousands.

Interactions

  • Conflict with Rome culminated in the Teutoburg Forest ambush (9 CE); subsequent Roman campaigns devastated the region.

What happened

Internal feuds and pressure from neighbors reduced the Cherusci; by late antiquity they were largely absorbed into emerging Saxon groupings.

Pannonians / Pannonian Illyrians (Danube basin, pre‑Roman to late Roman)
Closest distances
Pannonian (8.695), +Frank (4.89)
Identity
Pre‑Roman groups in the plains and hills of Pannonia (western Hungary, eastern Austria, northern Croatia/Slovenia/Serbia), later heavily Romanized.

Who and where

  • Mixed Illyrian and Celtic cultural zones along the middle Danube; province of Pannonia under Rome.

Population size

Multiple tribes across a large province, collectively likely in the hundreds of thousands during the Roman period.

Interactions

  • Provided soldiers and officers to the Roman army; later affected by Gothic, Hunnic, and Avar movements.

What happened

Through late antiquity, local populations were absorbed into shifting federations and later Slavic-speaking groups after the 6th–7th centuries.

The event

On 13 November 1002, King Æthelred II ordered the killing of Danish settlers in parts of England. A mass grave at Oxford contained young adult males with sharp‑force trauma and burning. Isotopes and genetics indicate Scandinavian origin. These individuals likely died in this purge and were hastily buried outside the city.

Cause of death: violent killing during a state‑ordered massacre; some remains show perimortem weapon injuries and charring.

What is an “elite Celtic burial”?

These 6th–5th century BCE “princely” graves of the Hallstatt/early La Tène culture contain high‑status individuals interred with wagons, feasting sets, jewelry, weaponry, and Mediterranean imports (e.g., bronze vessels). The mounds at Asperg and nearby Ludwigsburg in Baden‑Württemberg reflect a local power center connected to long‑distance trade across the Alps.

Cause of death: not specifically known; burials represent formal high‑status funerary rites rather than a violent event.

How these tribes connect and interact
  • Rhine corridor mixing: Nemetes and neighboring civitates became Romanized and later merged with Frankish groups. Your strongest composite match (Nemetes + Frank) fits this frontier blend.
  • Danube frontier links: Quadi and Pannonian communities were intertwined with Roman military networks and movements of peoples; composites with Franks and Cherusci point to West‑Germanic affinities plus Danubian inputs.
  • North Sea lineages: Matches to Viking‑Age individuals (Oxford, Denmark) reflect shared Northwest European ancestry streams that also fed into continental Franks and Saxons.
  • Celtic connections: Strong similarity to elite Hallstatt/La Tène burials in southwest Germany mirrors the deep Iron‑Age substrate in the Upper Rhine/Neckar region that later interfaced with Roman and Frankish populations.
Glossary and notes
  • Genetic distance: A mathematical measure of similarity of autosomal DNA to a reference; lower is closer.
  • mtDNA / Y‑DNA: Maternal and paternal uniparental markers. They represent single lines and do not summarize total ancestry.
  • Sample closeness %: A relative ranking versus other users; it is not a probability of descent.
